What Is an RNG?

A Random Number Generator (RNG) is a computational algorithm that produces a continuous stream of random numbers — thousands per second. When you press spin, the game uses the RNG's current output to determine the exact position of every symbol on the grid. The result is mathematically independent of every spin that came before it.

Pragmatic Play's RNG is a certified PRNG (Pseudo-Random Number Generator) — the same class of algorithm used in cryptography and financial systems. It passes statistical tests for randomness and is audited regularly by independent third parties including BMM Testlabs, GLI (Gaming Laboratories International), and QUINEL.

Can a Casino Make It Pay Less?

This is the nuanced part — and it's where "rigged" gets confused with "configured." Casinos cannot alter the RNG or change how a game functions. But Pragmatic Play, like most providers, offers games in multiple RTP configurations. Fury of Anubis may be available at 96.52%, 95.52%, or 94.52% depending on the operator's choice.

A casino choosing a reduced RTP version isn't "rigging" the game — they're selecting from a licensed, certified product range. It's disclosed in the game's information screen. Why Does It Feel Rigged?

The feeling of a rigged game almost always comes down to one of three things:

✗
Misunderstanding variance

Fury of Anubis triggers naturally 1 in 435 spins. At 3 spins/minute, that's ~145 minutes between average bonuses. Long dry spells are mathematically expected — they're not evidence of manipulation.

✗
Confirmation bias

We remember the 50-spin bonus drought vividly. We barely notice the 3-spin trigger that occasionally happens. The RNG doesn't care about your session history.

✗
Short-session RTP misconceptions

96.52% RTP is a long-run theoretical figure across billions of spins. In a 200-spin session you might return 30% or 400% — that's variance, not manipulation.
